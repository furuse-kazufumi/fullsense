A pun became a four-language mascot in 30 minutes.

My OSS project FullSense has a "plain-language edition" series — hard technical articles rewritten so anyone can follow them. In Japanese it's called *kamikudaki*: "chewed up for you."

Last night I mentioned a piece of wordplay to my AI assistant: *kamikudaki* echoes *kamitsuki* — written one way (噛みつき) it means "biting"; written another (神憑き), "divine possession."

In Japan, being bitten by a shishimai lion dancer brings good luck.

Thirty minutes later:

- The AI sketched 5 design directions as SVG and let me pick
- I gave exactly one reference: "like a classic irasutoya illustration"
- Final design: the lion bites the head of a reader holding a paper labeled "HARD" — and the bite grants the blessing of understanding
- It generated Japanese / English / Chinese / Korean variants, inserted them into 9 articles, and QA'd its own work with native-language reviewer agents plus headless-browser screenshots

My favorite detail: there are no performer's legs under the lion costume. Inside, there is only the spirit — a small golden orb with a circuit pattern, floating above the lion's head.

Japanese folklore says a tool gains a soul after 99 years of use. A few billion parameters? It might already be in there.

Humans bring cultural intuition and taste. AI brings exploration, production, and verification. That's what solo development looks like in 2026.
